The United States vs. AI: Copyright Under Judicial Fire

Summary: As the home of Big Tech, the United States presents a striking legal paradox. While federal law remains largely silent on AI, the courts and the Copyright Of ice have erected an impassable barrier: the human authorship requirement. Between rulings rejecting "machine-creators" and an avalanche of class actions contesting model training, the U.S. system has made Fair Use the cornerstone of the digital economy—potentially at the expense of the creators themselves.

I. The State of Black-Letter Law: Human Orthodoxy vs. Legislative Silence

U.S. Intellectual Property (IP) law is characterized by a conceptual rigidity that stands in stark contrast to the country’s technological flexibility. In the absence of specific AI legislation, a strict interpretation of founding texts prevails.

A. The categorical refusal of "Electronic Personhood"

While Title 17 of the U.S. Code (The Copyright Act) does not explicitly define an "author," the U.S. Copyright Office (USCO) and federal courts have filled this silence with an intangible doctrine: no human, no protection.

  • Copyright: The USCO systematically refuses to register works generated by a machine without human creative intervention (March 2023 Guidance). A purely algorithmic work falls into the public domain. If a human intervenes, they must disclose and disclaim the AI-generated portions to protect only their personal contribution.
  • Patents: Title 35 of the U.S. Code (The Patent Act) defines an inventor as an "individual," a term the Federal Court of Appeals interpreted as referring exclusively to a natural person (Thaler v. Vidal, 2022). Consequently, autonomous AI inventions are technically unpatentable.

B. The Battle over Training Data (Inputs)

This is the primary point of friction. Unlike the EU, the U.S. lacks a sui generis right for databases (Feist, 1991).

  • The Fair Use Gamble: The training of models on protected works relies entirely on the legal defense of Fair Use. Lacking a legislative Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception, developers (OpenAI, Google) argue that the massive ingestion of works to create a "transformative" model is lawful.
  • Legal Uncertainty: Because Fair Use is a fact-specific defense and not a bright-line rule, every use is a potential lawsuit. Non-original datasets are protected only by contract or trade secret law, leaving data creators vulnerable to massive scraping.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: Legislative Hesitation

While Congress holds frequent hearings, no major federal legislation has emerged, leaving the floor to transparency initiatives.

A. Toward Mandatory Disclosure?

The most significant proposal is the Generative AI Copyright Disclosure Act (H.R. 7913) of April 2024. This bill does not ban training but requires developers to file a notice listing the copyrighted works used in their datasets.

  • The Goal: To create traceability, allowing authors to assert their rights without the legislature having to prematurely decide on the legality of the training itself.

B. Reports and "Soft Law"

The USCO continues to issue prospective reports (2023–2025), notably suggesting federal protection for an individual's image and voice against "digital replicas" (deepfakes). However, on core issues like TDM exceptions, the administration is content to wait for the courts to clarify the application of Fair Use.

III. Current Litigation: Common Law in Action

The future of American AI is currently being written in the courtrooms. A series of historic trials is currently defining the boundaries of future law.

A. Confirming the Rejection of the AI-Author

The ruling in Thaler v. Perlmutter (D.C. Cir., March 2025) confirmed that an image generated autonomously by an AI ("A Recent Entrance to Paradise") cannot receive copyright. The court reminded the public that "human authorship is a bedrock requirement."

  • The Case to Watch: Allen v. Perlmutter (Colorado). This case tests the limits of "assisted" work. Artist Jason Allen, who used Midjourney with extensive iterations and post-processing for his award-winning "Théâtre D’opéra Spatial," is challenging the refusal to register his work. This case will determine the threshold of human intervention required for protection.

B. The Class Actions: The Trials of the Century?

Courts are seized by multiple class actions brought by artists and authors against AI giants. 1. Andersen v. Stability AI (California): The court allowed the infringement claim to proceed, finding it plausible that models contain compressed copies of training works. 2. Tremblay v. OpenAI: Authors (including Sarah Silverman) are suing over the "ingestion" of their books. While secondary infringement claims were dismissed for lack of "substantial similarity" in the outputs, the direct infringement (the act of training) remains the heart of the debate. 3. Doe v. GitHub: This litigation raises the novel question of AI compliance with Open Source licenses.

Conclusion

U.S. law is currently in a phase of "creative turbulence." By refusing to legislate hastily, the United States has tasked its judges with the Herculean effort of adapting 20th-century copyright law to the realities of generative AI. If the door remains closed to machine-authored works, the outcome of the Fair Use trials will determine whether America remains a "data paradise" for developers or shifts toward a mandatory licensing model.
