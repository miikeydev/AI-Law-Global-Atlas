AI in Kenya: Between Anthropocentric Orthodoxy and the Regulatory Vanguard

I. The State of Black-Letter Law: An Anthropocentric Orthodoxy

The current Kenyan legal framework, structured by the Copyright Act (Cap. 130) and the Industrial Property Act of 2001, rests on an unshakeable dogma: intellectual property is an emanation of human genius. However, Kenya distinguishes itself through a textual flexibility inherited from the British model.

A. The "Natural Person" Deadlock and the "Arranger" Exception

Kenyan copyright law conditions protection on the existence of an original human creation.

  • The Arranger Rule: For computer-generated works, the law provides a pragmatic legal fiction: the author is "the person by whom the arrangements necessary for the creation of the work are undertaken." This mechanism allows an AI production to be linked to a human (designer or user), provided they exercised significant control.
  • The "Sweat of the Brow" Criterion: Unlike French civil law, which requires the "stamp of the author's personality," Kenyan originality is assessed based on the "sufficient effort" invested. Yet, this threshold remains unreachable for a machine alone; without human intervention, a creation is deemed to lack originality and falls into the public domain.
  • The Refusal of the "Robot-Inventor": In patent law, the 2001 Act requires an inventor who has "actually conceived the invention." Kenya follows a strictly conservative approach here, refusing to recognize legal personhood for a machine, aligning with the global rejection seen in the DABUS case.

B. Databases and Algorithms: Protection by Default

Unlike the European Union, Kenya does not recognize sui generis rights for databases.

  • Protection through Compilation: A dataset is only protected if it constitutes an original compilation (selection or arrangement of data) requiring non-trivial intellectual labor. Raw data and facts remain free for appropriation.
  • The TDM Blind Spot: Substantive law is silent on the legality of data scraping for model training. In the absence of an explicit Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception, the massive use of protected works as algorithmic "fuel" constitutes a major legal risk for local developers.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: The 2030 Horizon

A. Clarifying the Status of Generative AI

The Kenya Copyright Board (KECOBO) and the Kenya Industrial Property Institute (KIPI) are collaborating on guidelines to frame the ownership of hybrid works. The ambition is two-fold: 1. To confirm the exclusion of work components produced autonomously by an AI. 2. To legitimize AI assistance by precisely defining the required level of human intervention (prompt selection, final editing).

B. The Remuneration and Transparency Project

An amendment to the Copyright Act is under review to resolve the machine learning dilemma. Two paths are being explored: the introduction of a TDM exception for research purposes, or the creation of a right to remuneration for authors whose catalogs feed AI models. This second option, supported by the music sector, signals a desire to protect the economic value of cultural heritage against global Tech giants.

C. The Rise of Ethical Regulation

While awaiting formal legislation, the Kenya Bureau of Standards (KEBS) released a draft AI Code of Conduct in April 2024. Although non-binding, this text promotes the traceability of training data and the documentation of algorithms, encouraging responsible self-regulation.

III. Current Litigation: The "Aryeh Movement" Jurisprudence

While the legislator bides its time, the Kenyan Copyright Tribunal took a stand in 2025 with a historic landmark decision.

A. Aryeh Movement Ltd v. Cynthia Beldina Akoth Okello (2025)

On September 26, 2025, the Tribunal rendered a fundamental judgment (COPTA/E001/2025) concerning literary works illustrated by AI.

  • The Ruling: The Tribunal solemnly affirmed that a work produced autonomously by an AI is excluded from all protection.
  • The Reasoning: In the absence of proof of a substantial original contribution from the human user, the AI cannot be considered an author. This decision constitutes the country's first operational judicial framework, harmonizing Kenyan law with U.S. (Thaler v. Perlmutter) and European standards.

B. Jurisprudential Silence and Data Privacy

Despite this breakthrough, Kenyan litigation remains silent on algorithmic patentability and platform liability for AI-generated infringement. However, attention is shifting toward personal data: the Kenyan firm Sama, a global hub for data annotation, has highlighted the ethical and contractual risks associated with the massive processing of content to train models like ChatGPT.

Conclusion: A Law in the Making

Kenya is navigating between rigor and adaptation. While the Aryeh Movement case secures the principle of the human author, the country still lacks a precise legislative framework for data scraping and the protection of trained models. The future of IP law in Kenya will depend on the state's ability to transform its strategic visions into binding norms. The stakes are high: preserving the nation's cultural sovereignty while providing the "Silicon Savannah" with the legal certainty necessary to become an African leader in Artificial Intelligence. The upcoming directives from KECOBO will be a decisive signal for investors and creators alike.
