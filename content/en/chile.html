Chile in the Age of AI: Between Anthropocentric Orthodoxy and Legislative Boldness

Summary: A land of innovation in Latin America, Chile currently finds itself at a legal crossroads. While its black-letter law remains firmly anchored in a humanist conception of intellectual property, de facto excluding algorithmic creations, the legislator took the initiative in 2024 with an ambitious bill. Between the introduction of a Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception and transparency requirements, Santiago is attempting to carve out a "third way": a regulation that secures model training without overturning the dogma of the human author.

I. The State of Black-Letter Law: The Human Author Fortress vs. the Data Vacuum

The current Chilean legal framework is characterized by a striking dichotomy: rigid clarity regarding ownership (outputs) and a risky, artistic blur regarding data exploitation (inputs).

A. The categorical refusal of artificial creativity

Chilean intellectual property law is built around an unshakeable paradigm: anthropocentrism.

  • Copyright: Law No. 17.336 (1970) and the Constitution reserve the status of "author" to the "natural person who creates." In the absence of a provision comparable to the UK Copyright Act, any work autonomously generated by an AI falls, by default, into the public domain. The lack of human creative contribution disqualifies the object from any protection, creating a legal "no-man's land" for purely algorithmic productions.
  • Patents: Law No. 19.039 on Industrial Property follows the same logic. Although the text does not explicitly exclude AI, administrative practice and international consensus (e.g., the DABUS case) mandate the designation of a human inventor. AI remains a tool, never a legal subject.
  • Trademarks: Only trademark law escapes this rigor, focusing on the distinctiveness of the sign rather than its creative origin.

B. The legal insecurity of model training

The real friction occurs upstream of creation. Unlike the European Union, Chile possesses neither a sui generis right for non-original databases nor a Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception.

  • The risk of mass infringement: Without an American-style Fair Use doctrine or a TDM exception, the massive ingestion of protected works to train models theoretically constitutes multiple copyright violations (reproduction and adaptation).
  • Economic vulnerability: Investments in the constitution of raw datasets do not benefit from specific proprietary protection. This leaves data producers defenseless against scraping, unless they resort to trade secrets or unfair competition law.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: Towards a Chilean "AI Act"?

Aware of these gaps, the government introduced Bill No. 16821-19 on May 7, 2024, marking a desire for pragmatic disruption.

A. The revolution of the TDM exception

The flagship measure of the bill lies in an amendment to Article 71 of the Copyright Law. This text would introduce a legal TDM exception allowing the use of protected works for automated analysis, provided there is lawful access.

  • Scope and Limits: While this measure aims to provide a "safe harbor" for AI developers, it is conditioned on the absence of disguised exploitation of the works. However, the text remains vague on the boundary between commercial and non-commercial use and—crucially—does not seem to provide an opt-out mechanism for authors, unlike the European directive.

B. Transparency and the status quo on authorship

The bill imposes a transparency obligation on AI providers, forcing them to disclose the datasets used. This measure aims to rebalance the scales by allowing rights holders to verify the use of their works. Conversely, the legislator refuses to touch the definition of the "author." The bill creates no new rights for AI creations, confirming the anthropocentric approach. Chile is choosing to regulate the tool (transparency, security) and the raw material (data) without personifying the machine.

III. Current Litigation: Watching the Global Battles

For now, silence reigns in Chilean courtrooms, but it is a "vigilant silence," fueled by the observation of foreign jurisprudence.

A. Absence of domestic case law

To date, no Chilean court has ruled on a dispute involving AI authorship or the legality of scraping. This jurisprudential void leaves stakeholders in an uncertainty that only the passage of the 2024 Bill can resolve.

B. The normative influence of foreign precedents

The Chilean legal system does not exist in isolation. Practitioners are integrating lessons from major international decisions: 1. Refusal of protection (USA/UK): Cases like Thaler v. Perlmutter (USA) and DABUS (UK) reinforce the Chilean position of denying copyright or patent status to AI. 2. Data litigation risks: US class actions (authors against OpenAI/Meta) and the Getty Images v. Stability AI case act as deterrents. The Chilean bill, with its TDM exception, appears as a legislative attempt to preemptively defuse the kind of mass litigation currently threatening the AI industry.

Conclusion

Chile is adopting a strategy of "conservative modernization." By preserving the central figure of the human author while opening the data floodgates through a legislative exception, Santiago seeks to become a sanctuary of legal certainty for innovation in Latin America. It remains to be seen whether the absence of an opt-out right for authors will eventually create major political friction with the cultural sector.
