Nigeria Confronting the Challenge of Artificial Intelligence: Between Legal Orthodoxy and Strategic Ambitions

Abstract: As Africa’s demographic and cultural giant, Nigeria has an intellectual property framework recently modernised by the Copyright Act 2022. Yet this statute remains silent on artificial intelligence, entrenching a resolutely anthropocentric approach that excludes autonomous creations. Between the lack of a Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception and the emergence of ambitious bills, this article analyses a legal system at a crossroads, where the dynamism of the creative scene (Nollywood, Afrobeats) may drive the emergence of novel case law.

I. State of Positive Law (law in force): The Bulwark of Legal Humanism

Nigerian positive law is marked by a misleading modernity. While the Copyright Act 2022 (in force since March 2023) reformed copyright law, it maintained—indeed reinforced—the dogma of human creation, leaving AI in a legal grey zone both as to ownership (outputs) and as to access to data (inputs).

A. The categorical refusal of “electronic personhood” (a debated concept of granting legal personality to AI systems)

Nigerian law makes human intervention a condition sine qua non for intellectual property protection.

  • Copyright: The 2022 Act recognises authorship only for “persons” (natural persons or legal persons representing humans). The requirement of originality resulting from a human intellectual effort (Section 2(2) of the Copyright Act) de facto excludes works generated autonomously by AI. Unlike the United Kingdom, which grants protection to “computer-generated works”, Nigeria provides no status for a “bionic entity”.
  • Patents: The Patents and Designs Act 1971, though ageing, points in the same direction. The statutory inventor is defined as a “person”, terminology which—combined with the obligation to state name and address—disqualifies DABUS-type AI systems.
  • The software grey zone: While software (and therefore AI source code) is protected as a literary work, abstract algorithms and mathematical methods remain excluded from patentability, absent a concrete technical application.

B. The absence of regulation for training data (Inputs)

This is arguably the most critical gap for the AI industry. Nigeria has neither an EU-style sui generis database right (a specific statutory right protecting a database maker’s substantial investment) nor a Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception.

  • Legal uncertainty for Machine Learning: In the absence of broad fair use or a specific exception, training models on protected works can, in principle, constitute copyright infringement.
  • Protection of databases: Only databases whose structure is original are protected by copyright. Compilations of raw or factual data (Section 3 of the Copyright Act) fall outside proprietary protection, leaving dataset creators with little recourse beyond trade secret protection or contract.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: Governance in the Making

Aware of the gap between positive law and technological reality, Nigeria is multiplying political and legislative initiatives, seeking to position itself as a regional leader in AI governance.

A. The legislative and strategic push

The year 2024 marked a turning point in political ownership of AI, even though IP-specific texts remain scarce.

  • Framework bills: The House of Representatives adopted at second reading, on 3 December 2024, a consolidated bill (including the National AI and Robotic Sciences Bill) aimed at creating a regulatory Commission. While these texts target overall governance, they lay the foundations for an authority capable of issuing future standards on data.
  • National Strategy (NAIS): Led by NITDA, this strategy under development should address rights ownership and ethics, filling the gap left by current laws.

B. Alignment with international diplomacy

Nigeria favours a multilateral approach, declining to legislate in isolation on the most complex IP issues.

  • Continental and global engagement: The country subscribed to the African Union’s Continental AI Strategy (June 2024) and to the Bletchley Park Declaration.
  • The NCC doctrine: The Nigerian Copyright Commission (NCC), through its Director-General in November 2024, acknowledged the need to find a “win-win” balance (licensing, remuneration), not excluding a revision of international norms. It already has extensive investigative powers enabling it to audit training databases where an infringement is suspected.

III. Litigation Developments: The Calm Before the Storm

While Nigerian courts remain silent on AI, local artistic practice and older case law sketch the contours of future disputes.

A. Case law by analogy

No decision has yet determined the fate of an AI-generated work. However, the principles established in Ifeanyi Okoyo v. Princess Printing (2005) and Yeni Anikulapo-Kuti (2014), which require “effort, skill and labour” from the author, leave little doubt: Nigerian judges would likely deny protection to a work lacking substantial human input.

B. Practical cases awaiting legal classification

The absence of proceedings does not mean the absence of conflicts. Nigeria’s music industry saw the release, in 2023, of an Afrobeats album generated by AI, and the Nollywood sector is experimenting with automated dubbing.

  • Litigation risk: These practices place creators in a legal fog—an inability to protect their AI productions and a risk of being sued for infringement if the AI has “ingested” protected works.
  • Foreign influence: Nigerian lawyers are tracking Western cases (such as Getty v. Stability AI or NYT v. OpenAI). It is highly likely that the first local disputes will draw on arguments developed in those jurisdictions, in particular on how infringement might be proven through analysis of AI outputs.

Conclusion

Nigeria stands at a pivotal stage. Its rigid, classical positive law offers an appearance of certainty but is ill-suited to the massive data flows of Machine Learning. By choosing, for now, the path of political strategy rather than a blunt reform of the intellectual property code, the Nigerian legislature is buying time for observation. Yet given the vitality of its cultural industries, that wait-and-see posture could quickly be overtaken by courtroom reality.
