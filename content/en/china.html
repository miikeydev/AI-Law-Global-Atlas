China and AI: Judicial Pragmatism as the Engine of Intellectual Property

Summary: Rather than simply absorbing the shock of the AI revolution, China is pursuing an assertive legal strategy. While lawmakers are laying the groundwork for a flagship AI statute, the courts have moved first—crafting an audacious body of case law. Between recognising copyright in AI-generated images and tightening obligations on model and platform providers, China is testing a system designed to reconcile the protection of creators with national technological ambition.

I. The Law as It Stands: Legislative Orthodoxy, Judicial Boldness

China’s legal framework reveals a striking duality: conventional statutes that do not explicitly address AI, and a judiciary willing to engage with it to fill the gaps.

A. The Human-Author Requirement

At the legislative level, China remains broadly aligned with international orthodoxy. The Copyright Law (2020) and patent legislation continue to presuppose human intervention.

  • Patents: The national office (CNIPA) has firmly rejected applications naming the AI system DABUS as inventor (decision of 29 December 2023), reiterating that only subjects recognised under civil law can hold inventor’s rights. The 2024 Examination Guidelines confirm this exclusion.
  • Copyright: The statute provides no protection for works created autonomously. Yet case law has introduced a crucial nuance: the emerging recognition of “AI-assisted works”.

B. The Case-Law Turn: Creative Investment as the Test

In contrast to the more rigid stance associated with the US Copyright Office, Chinese judges have taken a pragmatic approach as early as 2019.

  • Dreamwriter (2019): The Shenzhen court recognised protection for a finance article generated by AI, on the basis that Tencent’s teams had made a creative intellectual contribution through configuring the system.
  • Li v Liu (2023): The Beijing Internet Court made headlines by granting copyright protection for an image generated using Stable Diffusion. The judge emphasised that roughly 150 prompt iterations and parameter adjustments evidenced a human intellectual achievement, reducing the AI to the status of a mere tool.
  • The limit: Feng R. (2025): The Zhangjiagang court drew a welcome line by refusing protection where an image was produced from a single prompt, finding insufficient proof of an iterative creative process.

C. Regulating Training Data (Inputs)

China has no express text and data mining (TDM) exception in statute. Courts have, however, addressed the issue indirectly through the doctrine of temporary copying.

  • WAP Search (2013) and Tianjin (2024): Judges have treated transient copying in RAM, technically necessary for processing and lacking independent economic value, as falling outside infringement. The reasoning could, in practice, help stabilise AI training pending legislative intervention.
  • The 2023 Interim Measures: This administrative instrument requires AI providers to ensure the legality of their data sources, effectively creating an ex ante compliance obligation.

II. Reform Projects and Political Direction: Towards Codified Innovation

Beijing is no longer content to let judges carry the load alone; it is preparing a fuller legislative architecture.

A. A Dedicated AI Law

Listed on the State Council legislative agenda, a future AI statute—of which academic drafts have circulated since 2024—could introduce a statutory TDM exception for machine learning, bringing China closer to jurisdictions such as Japan or Singapore. It may also codify protection for AI-assisted works.

B. Platform Accountability Takes Centre Stage

The “Ultraman” (2024) dispute illustrates a growing willingness to regulate AI providers directly. The Guangzhou court held a provider liable for failing to prevent its system from generating infringing images of the well-known character. Relying on the 2023 Interim Measures, the court articulated a duty of vigilance—including measures such as labelling and filtering—effectively turning AI platforms into front-line guardians of intellectual property.

III. Live Litigation: A Global Legal Laboratory

In only a few years, China has become a theatre for some of the most forward-leaning decisions on AI anywhere.

A. The Originality Battle

Recent decisions (including those associated with Changshu and Zhangjiagang) sketch a nuanced doctrine: copyright protection is not automatic. It must be earned through proof of substantial human input—iterations, adjustments, retouching. Where the user merely “presses a button”, the output risks remaining legally ownerless.

B. The Specialisation of Internet Courts

Following an October 2025 reform by the Supreme People’s Court, the role of China’s Internet Courts has been recalibrated. Relieved of mass, routine disputes, they are now positioned to focus on complex litigation involving data and algorithms. The shift signals a coming wave of case law on the ownership of datasets and algorithm-driven unfair competition.

Conclusion

China is navigating with surprising agility. By using courts as legal sandboxes and regulating through administrative instruments (notably the 2023 Interim Measures), it has built an operational framework even before passing its flagship AI law. This hybrid model—protecting demonstrable human creative investment while imposing responsibility on platforms—may well influence Western lawmakers. China is no longer following international AI law; it is beginning to write it.
