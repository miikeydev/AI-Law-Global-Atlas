Brazil and the AI Challenge: Between Legal Vacuum and Praetorian Activism

Summary: As Brazil asserts itself as a major digital player in Latin America, its intellectual property framework is entering an unprecedented period of turbulence. Torn between strict anthropocentric legislation and the economic reality of generative models, Brazilian law oscillates between a rigid application of traditional principles and the emergence of bold reform projects. This is an analysis of a system where, while awaiting the legislator, the judges of the State of Santa Catarina are sketching the first lines of regulation through usage.

I. The State of Black-Letter Law: Human Authorship Orthodoxy vs. the "Gray Zone"

Brazilian substantive law is characterized by a hermetic refusal to recognize AI as a legal subject, creating a growing rift with technological practices.

A. The "Natural Person" deadlock

Brazilian copyright law is unequivocal. Law No. 9,610/1998 (Article 11) and the Constitution enshrine the principle that "the author is the natural person who creates the work." This provision de facto excludes autonomously generated AI creations from the scope of protection.

  • No protection for autonomous works: In the absence of identifiable human creative intervention, the work falls into the public domain. If AI is merely a tool steered by a human, protection is possible, but the degree of intervention required remains a zone of jurisprudential uncertainty.
  • Rejection of the non-human inventor: Regarding patents, the Industrial Property Law No. 9,279/1996 (LPI)requires the applicant to be a person. The Brazilian Patent and Trademark Office (INPI) clarified its doctrine in a 2022 opinion following the DABUS case, confirming the impossibility of naming an AI as an inventor. A human must be designated, even if they only configured the machine—a legal fiction that weakens the integrity of the patent system.

B. The vulnerability of training data (Inputs)

It is in the realm of data that the Brazilian framework proves most deficient compared to European standards.

  • Absence of sui generis rights: Unlike the EU, Brazil does not protect the investment in non-original databases. Only the creative structure is covered by copyright. Raw data and non-original datasets are therefore exposed to scraping, barring complex recourse to unfair competition law.
  • Theoretical illegality of training: Brazil possesses neither a Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception nor a "Fair Use" doctrine. Consequently, the massive ingestion of protected works to train models constitutes, according to the Ministry of Culture, a multiple violation of copyright (reproduction and adaptation). This legal insecurity places AI developers in a state of permanent risk.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: A Disruptive Legislation?

Aware of these impasses, the Brazilian legislator launched a major offensive in 2023-2024, seeking to position the country at the forefront of regulation.

A. Bill No. 2338/2023: Towards a new social pact for creation

Approved by the Senate on December 10, 2024, this ambitious text (the "Marco Legal" or Legal Framework) proposes an innovative balance between protection and innovation.

  • Transparency and Opt-out: The bill requires AI providers to declare the protected works used for training. Furthermore, it establishes an opt-out right for authors, returning control over the use of their creations to them.
  • Remuneration and Sandboxes: The text provides for a supervised negotiation environment to guarantee remuneration for rights holders, calculated based on the economic and competitive impact of the AI.
  • Structured Exceptions: A data mining exception is introduced but limited to non-commercial purposes (research, journalism)—a point of friction with the industry, which demands more flexibility.

B. Bill No. 303/2024: Bold controversy on patents

This text, still under discussion in the Chamber of Deputies (as of the October 2024 report), attempts a hybrid approach to inventiveness.

  • Modulated Protection: While maintaining the requirement for a human inventor, the bill proposes to drastically reduce the patent term (to 3 or 5 years) when AI has played a predominant role.
  • Doctrinal Criticism: This proposal raises serious concerns regarding its compliance with the TRIPS Agreement(which mandates a 20-year protection term) and risks creating legal uncertainty regarding the qualification of the AI’s role.

III. Current Litigation: Santa Catarina as a Judicial Laboratory

In the absence of promulgated law, the courts, particularly those of the State of Santa Catarina (TJSC),have taken the initiative to fill legal gaps with bold decisions.

A. The music royalties case (TJSC, August 2025)

In a resounding preliminary ruling opposing ECAD (the central collection bureau) and a theme park, the Santa Catarina Court of Justice ruled that the public performance of AI-generated music is subject to the payment of copyright royalties. Doctrinal Significance: The judge prioritized the protection of economic use over the identification of the author. Even without an identified human behind the creation, commercial exploitation generates a remuneration obligation. This is a de facto recognition of the proprietary value of artificial works.

B. The Meta / ANPD case (TJSC, March 2025)

This litigation illustrates the intersection between intellectual property and personal data. While the Public Prosecutor sought the suspension of Meta’s AI training (Llama) on user data, the TJSC deferred to the administrative decision of the ANPD (National Data Protection Authority). Analysis: In July 2023, the ANPD had already ordered a halt to this processing. This case demonstrates that, failing an effective lever in copyright law, the regulation of training data currently flows through Privacy channels, acting as an emergency brake against massive scraping.

Conclusion

Brazil stands at a crossroads. Its black-letter law, frozen in a classic humanist conception, no longer meets the challenges of generative AI. However, the current dynamic is one of vigorous reclamation: by the judiciary, which does not hesitate to tax AI usage, and by the legislator, who is sketching a transparent and remunerative regulatory model with Bill 2338. If these reforms succeed, Brazil could offer a credible alternative model to the European and American approaches.
