Singapore and AI: A Pragmatic Bet Between Intellectual Property Orthodoxy and Training Flexibility

Abstract

A major technology hub in Asia, Singapore has developed a distinctive legal strategy in response to artificial intelligence. Rather than embracing a new proprietary right over data, the city-state maintains a strict orthodox position on entitlement to rights (no protection without a human author or inventor), while simultaneously opening the innovation “floodgates” through some of the world’s most permissive Text and Data Mining (TDM) exceptions for model training. This article analyses a subtle balance between a “hands-off approach” to AI-generated creation and pro-business interventionism on training data.

I. The State of Positive Law: A Two-Speed Regime

Singapore’s positive law (i.e., the law in force) is characterised by a striking dichotomy: classical rigidity as to the protection of outputs (AI-generated creations), and bold flexibility as to inputs (training data).

A. The refusal of AI legal personality and the human requirement

Unlike the United Kingdom, which—through section 9(3) of the CDPA (Copyright, Designs and Patents Act 1988)—creates a legal fiction to protect computer-generated works, Singapore adheres to a strictly anthropocentric conception of copyright.

  • Exclusion of autonomous works: Case law is unequivocal. As early as the leading case Asia Pacific Publishing v Pioneers & Leaders (2011), the Court of Appeal held that identifying a human author is a condition sine qua non of protection (i.e., an indispensable condition). In the absence of legislation equivalent to the UK model, works generated autonomously by AI (texts, images, music) fall into the public domain (meaning: they are not protected by copyright). Where AI is used merely as a tool (“AI-assisted”), protection remains possible, but the burden of proving a genuine human creative contribution lies with the claimant.
  • Patent orthodoxy: Singapore’s Patent Act confines inventorship to the “natural person who devised the inventive concept” (natural person = human being, as opposed to a company). The Intellectual Property Office of Singapore (IPOS) applies international standards (in the vein of the DABUS line of cases), refusing to designate an AI system as inventor. Even where an innovation is patentable, it can only be so for the benefit of humans or legal persons (companies), provided that a flesh-and-blood inventor is named.

B. The Text and Data Mining (TDM) exception: an aggressive innovation lever

It is in the area of model training that Singapore stands out most sharply. The Copyright Act 2021 introduced a particularly broad exception for computational data analysis.

  • Scope of the exception: It permits copying and extracting data from protected works to which the user has “lawful access”. Notably, the exception expressly extends to commercial purposes, allowing businesses to train models without prior authorisation from rightsholders.
  • Mandatory character of the rule: The legislature has “hardened” the regime by making it mandatory: no contractual clause may restrict this exception. Accordingly, website terms and conditions purporting to prohibit scraping are ineffective where access is lawful, placing Singapore among the most machine-learning-friendly jurisdictions.
  • The database-law gap: Unlike the European Union, Singapore rejects a sui generis database right (a specific, standalone right protecting substantial investment in databases—an EU-only construct). In Global Yellow Pages Ltd v Promedia (2017), the Court of Appeal confirmed the rejection of the “sweat of the brow” doctrine (i.e., labour alone does not justify protection). A database is protected by copyright only if its structure is original. Purely factual Big Data compilations, or automatically generated datasets, are therefore, by default, unprotected—subject to reliance on trade secrets (confidential information regimes) or contract.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: The ‘Wait and See’ Doctrine

Singapore’s national AI strategy (2019) signals a commitment to supporting technology, but the legislature has adopted pragmatic caution, preferring observation to over-regulation.

A. Uncertainty over the status of AI-generated works

A lively doctrinal debate has emerged around recognising computer-generated works. In 2020, the Law Reform Committee of the Singapore Academy of Law (SAL) recommended adopting the UK model to fill the legal gap and encourage investment.

However, the government rejected that approach in the 2021 reform, opting for a “hands-off approach”. The policy choice is clear: allow the courts to draw the boundary case-by-case and wait for an international consensus before legislating. As of 2025, no bill seeks to grant specific rights to AI-generated creations.

B. Forward-looking adjustments on training data

While creating new property rights is ruled out (the 2020 SAL report advising against a property right in raw data), the scope of the TDM exception remains the subject of intense debate.

  • 2024 consultation: The Ministry of Law and IPOS opened a consultation on technological protection measures (TPMs/DRM). The issue is whether the TDM exception should allow the lawful circumvention of digital locks.
  • International pressure: Cultural industries (notably the Motion Picture Association) press for an opt-out mechanism, fearing Singapore could become a “data-mining haven” where global works are exploited without compensation. The government is thus at a crossroads: preserve its attractiveness for AI developers or offer stronger assurances to rightsholders.

III. Litigation Developments: Calm Before the Storm?

An examination of judicial activity reveals a paradox: while theoretical questions abound, AI-specific litigation remains almost non-existent.

A. The reach of landmark cases

Singaporean case law rests on two pillars that effectively lock in the current system:

  1. Asia Pacific Publishing (2011): This foundational Court of Appeal decision entrenched the requirement of a human author. It operates as a preventive safeguard, likely discouraging attempts to claim rights in purely artificial outputs (e.g., Midjourney or DALL-E-type content) before local courts.
  2. Global Yellow Pages (2017): By refusing to protect factual databases through copyright, the Court of Appeal confirmed that financial or technical investment alone does not create a monopoly.

B. The absence of mass litigation

Unlike the United States, Singapore does not (yet) face class actions by artists against AI generators.

Legal analysis: This judicial silence can be explained structurally by the robustness of the 2021 TDM exception. Whereas US fair use is a case-by-case defence subject to judicial balancing, the Singaporean exception is a clear and broad rule of positive law. Rightsholders therefore have limited room to challenge model training on infringement grounds (copyright infringement), so long as access to the underlying data is lawful.

However, commentators note a risk of cross-border disputes. Issues akin to “double actionability” (a conflict-of-laws test requiring the conduct to be actionable in both the forum and the place of the tort) could arise if a company exploits, in Singapore, data protected abroad while seeking shelter behind the more permissive local regime.

Conclusion

Singapore offers a fascinating legal laboratory. By refusing to personify AI or to create complex sui generis rights, the city-state prioritises legal certainty and contractual fluidity. If the human creator remains the only “sacred” subject of rights, the regulatory environment is deliberately designed to make the island a sanctuary for algorithm training—even at the cost of friction with traditional cultural industries.
