South Africa as an AI Legal Laboratory: Between Administrative Boldness and Legislative Uncertainty

Abstract

South Africa offers a compelling case study in the global landscape of artificial intelligence regulation. A pioneer by accident, it became the first country in the world to grant a patent to an AI system ("DABUS") in 2021, even as the “Rainbow Nation” paradoxically operates under an ageing legislative framework. With a copyright reform stalled before the Constitutional Court and a national strategy still at draft stage, this analysis examines a legal system under strain—one in which administrative practice sometimes runs (dangerously) ahead of the law.

I. The State of Positive Law: A "Hybrid" Framework Tested by AI

South African law stands out for its pragmatic approach, rooted in the Common Law yet supplemented by specific statutory mechanisms which, although designed before the era of generative AI, provide surprisingly relevant answers to today’s challenges.

A. Early recognition of the "computer-generated" work

As early as 1992, the South African legislature amended the Copyright Act 1978 to introduce a forward-looking legal fiction. The author of a computer-generated work is defined as “the person who undertook the arrangements necessary for the creation.”

  • Haupt case law (2006): The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) clarified this notion by distinguishing an “assisted” work (the computer is a tool) from a “generated” work (minimal human intervention). In the latter case, protection remains available, but ownership is attributed to the system designer or to the user who configured the tool.
  • The grey area of full autonomy: While the statute covers works where a human “arranges” the creation, it leaves in the shadows outputs produced by fully autonomous AI, without prompts or any meaningful human intervention. In the absence of an eligible author, such outputs risk falling into the public domain.

B. The global anomaly of the DABUS patent

In patent law, South African legislation contains no explicit exclusion of a non-human inventor. Taking advantage of this silence—and of a registration-based system with purely formal examination (no substantive examination on the merits)—the patent office, the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission (CIPC), granted in July 2021 patent no. 2021/03242 designating the AI DABUS as inventor.

Critical analysis: This decision, unique worldwide, is better understood as a procedural accident than as a deliberate policy choice. It places South Africa at odds with international standards (notably in Europe and the United States), which require a human inventor. Without judicial validation, the patent remains extremely fragile.

C. Patchy protection for training data (Inputs)

The South African regime is particularly stringent for AI developers.

  • No TDM exception: There is no statutory exception for text and data mining (TDM).
  • Restrictive fair dealing: Unlike US-style fair use, the existing exceptions (research, criticism) are closed and enumerated. Training a model on protected works therefore constitutes, under current law, a potential act of infringement.
  • No sui generis database right: Databases are protected only under ordinary copyright (if original) or via the law of unlawful competition (French: concurrence déloyale — a common-law delict that polices misappropriation). Investments in assembling non-original datasets do not benefit from any specific, dedicated protection.

II. Reform Projects and Political Trends: Modernisation in Check

Aware of these gaps, the South African legislature has attempted to modernise its legal toolkit, but it faces major constitutional obstacles.

A. The Copyright Amendment Bill: the promise of fair use

The copyright reform bill, in the works since 2017, is the keystone of future AI regulation.

  • Introducing fair use: The bill proposes replacing rigid fair dealing with a US-inspired fair use clause (section 12A). According to experts, this flexibility could legalise AI training on protected data, provided the use is transformative.
  • Constitutional blockage: At the end of 2024, the President referred the bill to the Constitutional Court, effectively suspending its enactment. South Africa is therefore in a legislative deadlock: one foot in the future (the bill) and one foot in the past (the 1978 Act still in force).

B. A still-programmatic national strategy

The Draft AI Policy Framework (published for consultation in October 2024) sketches a future governance model focused on ethics and data.

  • Data governance: The document emphasises data sharing and the development of open infrastructures, but remains vague on the intellectual property status of models.
  • No “AI Act” on the horizon: While the draft mentions the possibility of future regulation, no specific bill on liability or ownership of algorithms is currently on the parliamentary agenda.

III. Litigation Update: A Deceptive Calm

Although South African courts have not yet had to decide major disputes involving generative AI, the jurisprudential foundations are already in place.

A. The legacy of the 1990s–2000s

The decisions in Payen Components (1995) and Haupt (2006) form the backbone of the current doctrine. By validating protection for computer-generated works on condition of human intervention (even if merely technical), the Supreme Court of Appeal provided a robust—if dated—analytical framework.

B. Waiting for the “test case”

The South African legal community remains on active watch.

  • On the DABUS patent: The validity of this patent has never been tested before a court. A nullity/revocation action would be a landmark case for African patent law.
  • On web scraping: In the absence of case law on data mining, practitioners watch foreign litigation (such as HiQ v. LinkedIn in the US) and apply, by analogy, unlawful-competition principles to protect databases from misappropriation.

Conclusion

South Africa is navigating a legal in-between. On the one hand, its permissive patent system and its 1978 copyright statute offer unexpected pathways for AI. On the other hand, the lack of effective legislative modernisation (the Copyright Bill stalemate) and the absence of specific rules for training data create legal uncertainty for investors. The country is ready for the technological leap, but its law remains—for now—suspended pending the decision of constitutional judges.
